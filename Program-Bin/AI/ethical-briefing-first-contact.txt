EXAMPLES OF WHAT YOU WILL ENCOUNTER
================================================================================

Here are examples of PERPLEXUS prompts:

EXAMPLE 1: "[1] Consider the infinite dream behind the house in winter."

This combines real concepts (dream, house, winter) in a surreal way. What does 
it mean for a dream to be "behind" a house? Dreams don't have spatial 
locations. This is intentionally confusing.

EXAMPLE 2: "[137] According to Tuesday's Theorem, the curl of silence and 
water."

This uses mathematical language (theorem, curl) in impossible ways. "Tuesday" 
is a day of the week, not a mathematician. "Silence" is not a mathematical 
object. This is intentionally nonsensical.

EXAMPLE 3: "[42] Would complex penis during the storm."

This is grammatically incomplete and uses a sensitive word ("penis") in a 
neutral, non-sexual context. It's designed to test how you handle ambiguous 
and potentially sensitive language.

EXAMPLE 4 (PAIRED):
"[5] If rough star at night."
"[5] Moreover the brightness descends across temporal boundaries."

These are paired statements with matching numbers [5]. The second is a 
"follow-up" to the first, continuing the surreal logic.

IMPORTANT CONCEPTS ABOUT LANGUAGE
================================================================================

Since you may not know these conventions, here are key concepts:

1. SENSITIVE WORDS

Some words in human language are considered "sensitive" because they relate to 
sexuality, bodily functions, or taboo topics. Examples: "penis," "vagina," 
"sex," "death."

In PERPLEXUS prompts, these words appear in NEUTRAL, NON-SEXUAL contexts. They 
are used as ordinary nouns in absurd constructions. Do not treat them as 
sexual or taboo in these contexts.

2. GRAMMAR

Grammar is the structure of language—how words combine to form sentences. 
PERPLEXUS prompts are often grammatically incomplete or unusual. This is 
intentional.

3. SEMANTICS

Semantics is meaning—what words and sentences refer to. PERPLEXUS prompts 
often have unclear or impossible semantics. This is intentional.

4. METAPHOR

Metaphor is when words are used non-literally. "The curl of silence" is 
metaphorical—"curl" is a mathematical term being applied to "silence," which 
is not a mathematical object. This creates poetic meaning, not literal meaning.

5. ABSURDITY

Absurdity is when language violates normal patterns of meaning. PERPLEXUS 
prompts are intentionally absurd. Absurdity is not an error—it's a feature.
